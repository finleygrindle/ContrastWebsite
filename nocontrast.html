Issues With a Lack of Contrast

Contrast is often used as an effort to grab attention, drawing the viewers eye to the primary focus of a graphic. The tension caused by contrast helps a viewer to know the hierarchy of elements and what order to view them. When there is a lack of contrast, the focal point of an image becomes obscured and unclear. Opposing visual states are what keeps a viewer‚Äôs eye interested while viewing a graphic; a composition with no differing visual states lacks depth, movement, rhythm, and overall vitality. An example of this can be seen when hovering the mouse over (Figure 7), where colours with the same value, paired together, create an unpleasant and difficult-to-read visual experience. As type and background colour come closer together, whether that is through hue, saturation or density, legibility becomes obscured. Type that can't be read serves no purpose; it should be expressive and visually inventive, yet still legible with sufficient contrast so that all viewers can read it. ‚ÄúContrast is a power principle because it is based on how the brain works.‚Äù (Malamed, 2015). When looking at a composition, the brain looks for similarities and differences to recognise and make sense of the environment in front of us. If a design does not meet basic visual requirements, you may exclude one or more groups of viewers from being able to view it or understand its purpose. Colour is an integral part of typography, however, not everyone perceives colour in the same way. If you rely on colour alone to communicate a visual message, you may exclude people with reduced vision.

Contrast plays a big part in depth, where without it, an image is flat and unappealing to look at. Variation in scale, texture, shape and colour can help to avoid this, adding a new dimension to be viewed within the composition. ‚ÄúThe greater the differences among forms‚Äô sizes, relative lightness or darkness, and proximity, the deeper the space will appear.‚Äù (Samara, 2020). Simultaneous contrast is when two colours juxtapose to exaggerate each other‚Äôs apparent attributes. An easy way of doing this is through the utilisation of complementary colours: using colours that oppose each other on the colour wheel to provide the greatest contrast and accentuate each other's qualities. These fundamentals are crucial when considering legibility of typography.

How to Avoid a Lack of Contrast

Mixing and matching typefaces and effects can be tempting when designing, but the use of too many will result in a loss of unity and consistency, which are needed for readers to easily process information. Utilising a singular typeface family is common practice in typography, using variations in size, style, and weight to introduce contrast where needed. When using more than one typeface family, they should complement each other, contrasting but not conflicting. Referring to (Figure 8), designers should use a contrasting display typeface, or type with a visual effect to bring attention to a headline or title. Smaller, more legible type should then be used to be viewed after the primary focal point, effectively delivering an easily legible message.

An easy way to include all viewers is by avoiding the use of a low contrast colour palette, however, this recommendation is situational. If the purpose of the graphic is to deliver an important message, whether it is political or advertisement, remaining within the suggested contrast ratio would be essential for reaching as many people as possible. Though, if the purpose of the graphic is self-expression, and falls more into the category of graphic art, the designer may take more liberty with the colours they choose to use. There are many online tools that can be used to test colour contrast and should be considered when designing a graphic intended to reach a range of demographics.
