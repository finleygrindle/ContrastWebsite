Contrast Through Dimensionality

An effective, yet lesser-known way to manipulate a graphic space when working with typography is to create the illusion of depth. Type can respond to scale, directional movement, and tonal variation, relating to an image by virtue of mimicry of depth and perspective (Samara, 2011). Text layers and shapes are confined to a two-dimensional plane, meaning that they are composed of width and height, but not depth. However, their two-dimensionality can be used to define layouts and compose elemental form. Art is nothing without form, and form is achieved by integrating depth into a shape or type layer, encapsulating volume by creating three dimensions in a graphic. Three-dimensionality can be achieved through a variety of ways, such as linear perspective, occlusion, varying object sizes, colour, texture details, shadows, spatial depth, and atmospheric depth. An important part of creating this illusion is through proximity and layering, taking advantage of the human ability to see three dimensions, even on a two-dimensional plane. Through utilising this, you can obtain a whole new level of visual possibilities, creating potential to enhance environmental storytelling and add realism where needed.

The effective use of minimalism preserves easy viewing; if all elements contrast, the composition can become overwhelming.

Contrast in Layout

In order to ensure a rich, engaging optical experience, a designer must compose elements in a way that has harmonic rhythm among all the layout's aspects. Harmonic rhythm is another way of saying tension, when all elements come together and relate to each other. “During the design process, work with the layout to create a relationship between foreground and background so that it looks and feels harmonious.” (Malamed, 2015). Layouts are especially important when the design is conveying an important message, where the audience is not only required to recognise and comprehend the information, but to also retain it. Furthermore, it is essential when the information is meant to be perceived quickly and clearly, for example, a billboard viewed by people driving past. Two effective layout techniques are establishing a visual hierarchy or working with a grid. Visual hierarchy involves making the most important content the most dominant feature, by varying the size of elements or aligning them to the upper left of the layout (the typical visual entry point). A successful hierarchy provides a clear focal point, so that the viewer knows where to look first.

However, some elements must contrast each other to individually appreciate them more clearly. An easy way to create this contrast is through the use of a grid: composing a layout, then breaking it to create a point of visual interest and emphasise key information to be perceived by the viewer. A grid can help to plan a composition that utilises white space effectively and innovatively to create sufficient irregularities, providing structure in a space through the considered placement of elements. They can come in many forms, but at their core are used to separate a graphic into horizontal and vertical sections. (Figure 2) utilises a 3x4 grid, creating symmetry and asymmetry simultaneously, which are both as important as each other in typography. The two should be used in harmony to produce a clear and communicative message that is both easy to understand and visually interesting. A symmetrical configuration can be very direct and efficient, but too simple or static, causing viewers to become bored and not intake the present information. Asymmetrical arrangements foster involvement; the differences stimulate the eye to move more. The amount of symmetry and asymmetry needed can vary between projects, depending on which association will be most appropriate for the target audience and message being conveyed. An important aspect of typography is to arrange type in a way that is conductive to the designs purpose.
