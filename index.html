Contrast Through Dimensionality

An effective, yet lesser-known way to manipulate a graphic space when working with typography is to create the illusion of depth. Type can respond to scale, directional movement, and tonal variation, relating to an image by virtue of mimicry of depth and perspective (Samara, 2011). Text layers and shapes are confined to a two-dimensional plane, meaning that they are composed of width and height, but not depth. However, their two-dimensionality can be used to define layouts and compose elemental form. Art is nothing without form, and form is achieved by integrating depth into a shape or type layer, encapsulating volume by creating three dimensions in a graphic. Three-dimensionality can be achieved through a variety of ways, such as:

+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ scale
+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ colour
+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ texture
+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ shadows
+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ spatial depth
+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ atmospheric depth

An important part of creating this illusion is through proximity and layering, taking advantage of the human ability to see three dimensions, even on a two-dimensional plane. Through utilising this, you can obtain a whole new level of visual possibilities, creating potential to enhance environmental storytelling and add realism where needed.

(Figure 1) serves as a well-informed display of contrast while maintaining simplicity and a limited colour palette. Atmospheric depth has been used through contrasting values and overlapping to create a sense of foreground and background while keeping both type forms the same size. The use of positive and negative space also adds to this sense of foreground, effectively creating visual interest; when negative and positive space are equal, there is a lack of spatial depth, and the design becomes visually static. This is necessary when wanting to create depth without inherently making one element appear further away than another. A contrast in type size is also used to further the idea of tension, with smaller type being used to convey a simplistic message about typography that the viewer would then read after viewing the main subject of the graphic. The effective use of minimalism preserves easy viewing; if all elements contrast, the composition can become overwhelming.

Contrast in Layout

In order to ensure a rich, engaging optical experience, a designer must compose elements in a way that has harmonic rhythm among all the layout's aspects. Harmonic rhythm is another way of saying tension, when all elements come together and relate to each other. “During the design process, work with the layout to create a relationship between foreground and background so that it looks and feels harmonious.” (Malamed, 2015). Layouts are especially important when the design is conveying an important message, where the audience is not only required to recognise and comprehend the information, but to also retain it. Furthermore, it is essential when the information is meant to be perceived quickly and clearly, for example, a billboard viewed by people driving past. Two effective layout techniques are:

+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ establishing a visual hierarchy
+‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ ‎ working with a grid

Visual hierarchy involves making the primary content the most dominant feature, by varying the size of elements or aligning them to the upper left of the layout (the typical visual entry point). A successful hierarchy provides a clear focal point, so that the viewer knows where to look first.

However, some elements must contrast each other to individually appreciate them more clearly. An easy way to create this contrast is through the use of a grid: composing a layout, then breaking it to create a point of visual interest and emphasise key information to be perceived by the viewer. A grid can help to plan a composition that utilises white space effectively and innovatively to create sufficient irregularities, providing structure in a space through the considered placement of elements. They can come in many forms, but at their core are used to separate a graphic into horizontal and vertical sections. (Figure 2) utilises a 3x4 grid, creating symmetry and asymmetry simultaneously, which are both as important as each other in typography. The two should be used in harmony to produce a clear and communicative message that is both easy to understand and visually interesting. A symmetrical configuration can be very direct and efficient, but too simple or static, causing viewers to become bored and not intake the present information. Asymmetrical arrangements foster involvement; the differences stimulate the eye to move more. The amount of symmetry and asymmetry needed can vary between projects, depending on which association will be most appropriate for the target audience and message being conveyed. An important aspect of typography is to arrange type in a way that is conductive to the designs purpose.

Contrast Through Elements

An element can be lots of things in graphic design, including images, shapes, type, and even the graphic space itself. A graphic space should be seen as more than just a container for your design: it is the area where you organise and manipulate typography to communicate visually. An effective design can be broken down into two parts: key visual elements and background. “Too many visual elements can make a layout look and feel cluttered” (Malamed, 2015). A way this can be easily avoided is by removing unnecessary elements or grouping smaller elements together. A designer can easily sort through the essential visual elements by considering which are adding to the message of the graphic; if it is not adding to the message, it is more likely subtracting from it. Creating contrast among these visual elements is key, ensuring tension and liveliness within a composition. Viewers often find flat compositions boring as they do not impart a sense of depth or movement, resulting in a dull and lifeless design. An exploitation of variation can fool the viewer into seeing depth, even when it is not there, to convince them that they’re looking at a “window into a bigger dimensional world” (Samara, 2020).

Randomness and variety should only be used intentionally, and when done correctly, can create a design that is both logical and interesting. (Figure 3) is a blurry composition of muddled letters that spell “contrast” in large writing. The design draws attention to the message on an appropriate scale, utilising subtle, limited colours that work together and show clear intention. In most instances, large type utilising a visual effect is best used as a design element that is not intended to function legibly, but to draw attention to the real message behind the graphic. There is a randomness to the type that is scrambled across the screen with a motion blur effect, while maintaining a clear and easily understood theme. A designer should only introduce an obvious irregularity where they wish to make a distinction, as irregularities will always be interpreted as significant by the viewer. (Figure 3) also demonstrates consistency through the imagery and effects used. This is essential when considering visual effects in a graphic; anything that does not add to the composition contributes no meaning. A well-made graphic features a single visual voice, where elements talk and relate to each other to add to the totality of the design and strengthen the message. Decisively applied contrast in typography can create optical pushing and pulling between the on-screen elements. Contrast Through Typefaces

As previously stated, it is not essential to use more than one typeface family to create contrast and provide dynamic variation within typography. Modern graphic design is fundamentally rooted in the use of typefaces to convey a message, before imagery could be used in print. Legible type is good type, sometimes it only takes a simple typeface to effectively communicate something. For example, (Figure 4) only uses Neue Haas Grotesk, but utilises it in multiple different weights of the font family. The message is still clearly read, while allowing room for variation and visual interest. Designers should always prioritise a typeface that aids legibility, and it is common practice to use a maximum of two typefaces within a graphic. In some instances, three is appropriate, however, a change in typeface usually signals a change in function. Following the rule of only two will always help to maintain visual consistency and ensure that all elements are harmonious in some kind of way; a second typeface can be nice for contrast, but too many are distracting and overwhelming for the viewer.

As stated in Typography in design (Bobeico, 2024), serif typefaces represent a distinguished subset of type, widely embraced in the composition of literary works and bodies of text. They can also be seen in more digital domains, such as corporate identities and branding with a pursuit of tradition, aiming to invoke a sense of professionalism and trustworthiness. Whereas sans serif fonts, such as the one seen in (Figure 4), provide a clean aesthetic, achieving a direct visual approach, often through consistency of stroke weight. These typefaces should be used to communicate qualities of simplicity, leaving more room for the design itself to flourish. “Letters can be expressive when used alone, as a simple silhouette, as an outline, or as a container for image, texture or pattern. ... Its inherent integration unifies the design of the whole piece.” (Saltz, 2009). Adding details to type can provide an extra level of meaning or make the design more specific to the message; in (Figure 4), the message is contrast. Neue Haas Grotesk is a typeface that exemplifies neutrality, allowing it to be used in a plethora of contexts and assume the identity of a variety of projects. In this graphic the strategy relies on decisive choices for the size and weight of text elements to create visual contrast that intertwines with the stylistic unity of the composition.
